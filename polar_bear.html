Overview

The polar bear (Ursus maritimus) is a large marine mammal classified as a carnivore and a bear native to the Arctic. It is the largest land carnivore and excellent swimmers, adapted to life on sea ice. Listed as Vulnerable due to climate change and habitat loss from sea ice reduction.

Characteristics

Physical Traits

White fur for camouflage, thick blubber layer for insulation, large paws for gripping ice, and powerful swimming limbs. Adults weigh 350-700 kg with males significantly larger than females.

Diet & Hunting

Primary diet consists of seal blubber, hunted from sea ice. Uses breathing holes and waits patiently for seals to surface. Can consume up to 100 kg in a single feeding session.

Habitat Requirements

Entirely dependent on sea ice for survival. Climate change reducing ice coverage poses existential threat. Range extends across 19 circumpolar populations in the Arctic.

Behavior

Solitary hunters with exceptional sense of smell. Females den during winter, giving birth to 1-3 cubs. Cubs remain with mother for 2.5-3 years.

Ecological Significance

Polar bears are apex predators in Arctic food chains and serve as indicators of ocean health and climate trends. Their presence reflects the integrity of Arctic marine ecosystems.
