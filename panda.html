Overview

The giant panda (Ailuropoda melanoleuca) is a large bear-like mammal endemic to China, recognized by its distinctive black-and-white coloring. Listed as Vulnerable, pandas primarily inhabit mountain forests and are almost entirely dependent on bamboo as their food source.

Characteristics

Physical Traits

Large bear with distinctive black and white pelage. Weight ranges from 75-135 kg. Round face, black eye patches, black ears, and black limbs. The "thumb" (radial sesamoid bone) helps grip bamboo stalks.

Diet & Lifestyle

99% of diet consists of bamboo, consuming 12-15 kg daily. Despite being carnivores, pandas are perfectly adapted to herbivory. Spend 12-16 hours daily eating due to bamboo's low nutritional value.

Habitat & Range

Found exclusively in central China, in isolated mountain forests at elevations of 1,200-3,400 meters. Habitat loss and fragmentation pose serious threats. Protected within several nature reserves.

Behavior & Reproduction

Generally solitary except during mating season. Females give birth to 1-3 cubs but typically raise only one. Cubs remain with mother for 2-3 years. Excellent climbers despite their size.

Conservation Efforts

The giant panda is a symbol of wildlife conservation. Intensive breeding programs and habitat protection in China have improved populations. In 2016, the IUCN reduced its status from Endangered to Vulnerable, marking a significant conservation success.
