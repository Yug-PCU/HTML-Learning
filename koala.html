Overview

The koala (Phascolarctos cinereus) is a marsupial native to Australia, often mistaken for a bear due to its appearance. Listed as Vulnerable, koalas are arboreal herbivores found exclusively in eucalyptus forests along the eastern and southeastern coasts of Australia.

Characteristics

Physical Traits

Stocky, gray marsupial weighing 4-15 kg. Distinctive features include fluffy ears, dark nose, and dense fur. Strong limbs with sharp claws suited for gripping tree branches. Despite similarity to bears, koalas are marsupials, not bears.

Diet & Lifestyle

Exclusively folivorous—eating only eucalyptus leaves. Select from over 600 eucalyptus species but eat only a few dozen. Sleep 18-22 hours daily due to low-energy diet. Toxic eucalyptus leaves are detoxified by specialized liver enzymes.

Habitat & Distribution

Entirely arboreal, living in eucalyptus forests along eastern Australia. Range extends from tropical north to cool southern forests. Habitat loss and fragmentation threaten populations significantly.

Reproduction & Development

Females produce single joeys annually. Newborn remains in pouch for 6 months, then rides mother's back for another 6 months. Reach sexual maturity at 2-3 years. Can live 10-12 years in wild.

Conservation Challenges

Koalas face severe threats from habitat loss, climate change, bushfires, and disease. Recent Australian wildfires devastated populations, particularly in Queensland and New South Wales. Conservation efforts focus on habitat protection and research into chlamydial disease affecting wild populations.
